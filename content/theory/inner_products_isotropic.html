Isotropic Constitutive Relationships¶

Summary¶

A constitutive relationship quantifies the response of a material to an external stimulus. Examples include Ohm’s law and Hooke’s law. For practical applications of the finite volume method, we may need to take the inner product of expressions containing constitutive relationships.

Let \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) be two physically related quantities. If their relationship is isotropic (defined by a constant \(\sigma\)), then the constitutive relation is given by:

(1)¶\[\vec{v} = \sigma \vec{w}\]

Here we show that for isotropic constitutive relationships, the inner product between a vector \(\vec{u}\) and the right-hand side of equation (1) is approximated by:

\[(\vec{u}, \sigma \vec{w} ) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \sigma \vec{w} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M w}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{M}\) represents an inner-product matrix, and vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) are discrete variables that live on the mesh. It is important to note a few things about the inner-product matrix in this case:

  1. It depends on the dimensions and discretization of the mesh

  2. It depends on where the discrete variables live; e.g. edges, faces

  3. It depends on the spacial variation of the material property \(\sigma\)

For this class of inner products, the corresponding inner product matricies for discrete quantities living on various parts of the mesh are given by:

\[\begin{split}\textrm{Vectors on faces:} \; \boldsymbol{M_{\sigma f}} &= \frac{1}{4} \boldsymbol{P_f^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \sigma ) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_f} \\ \textrm{Vectors on edges:} \; \boldsymbol{M_{\sigma e}} &= \frac{1}{4^{k-1}} \boldsymbol{P_e^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \sigma) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_e}\end{split}\]

where

  • \(k = 1,2,3\) represent the dimension (1D, 2D or 3D)

  • \(\boldsymbol{e_k}\) is a vector of 1s of length \(k\)

  • \(\odot\) is the Hadamard product

  • \(\otimes\) is the kronecker product

  • \(\boldsymbol{P_f}\) and \(\boldsymbol{P_e}\) are projection matricies that map quantities from faces and edges to cell centers, respectively

  • \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is a vector that stores all of the volumes of the cells

  • \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\) is a vector containing the physical property values for the cells

Tutorial: To construct the inner product matrix and/or approximate inner products of this type, see the tutorial on inner products with constitutive relationships

Vectors on Cell Faces¶

We want to approximate the inner product between a vector quantity \(\vec{u}\) and the product of \(\sigma\) and \(\vec{w}\). Here, we discretize such that \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) are defined to live on cess faces. Our goal is to construct the inner product matrix \(\boldsymbol{M}\) in the expression below:

(2)¶\[(\vec{u}, \sigma \vec{w}) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \sigma \vec{w} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M \, w}\]

We must respect the dot product. For vectors defined on cell faces, we discretize such that the x-component of the vectors live on the x-faces, the y-component lives y-faces and the z-component lives on the z-faces. For a single cell, this is illustrated in 2D and 3D below. By decomposing the domain into a set of finite cells, we assume the physical properties are spacial invariant within each cell.

As we can see there are 2 faces for each component. Therefore, we need to project each component of the vector from its faces to the cell centers and take their averages separately. For a single cell with volume \(v_i\) and material property value \(\sigma_i\), the contribution towards the inner product is:

(3)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \mathbf{In \; 2D:} \; \int_{\Omega_i} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv \approx & \;\; \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_x^{(1)} + u_x^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_x^{(1)} + w_x^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_y^{(1)} + u_y^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_y^{(1)} + w_y^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & \\ \mathbf{In \; 3D:} \; \int_{\Omega_i} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv \approx & \;\; \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_x^{(1)} + u_x^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_x^{(1)} + w_x^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_y^{(1)} + u_y^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_y^{(1)} + w_y^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_z^{(1)} + u_z^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_z^{(1)} + w_z^{(2)} \Big ) \end{align}\end{split}\]

where superscripts \((1)\) and \((2)\) denote face 1 and face 2, respectively. Using the contribution for each cell described in expression (3), we want to approximate the inner product in the form described by equation (2). To accomlish this, we construct a sparse matrix \(\boldsymbol{P_f}\) which projects quantities on the x, y and z faces separately to the the cell centers.

For discretize vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) whose x, y (and z) components are organized on cell faces as follows:

\[\begin{split}\boldsymbol{u} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{u_x} \\ \boldsymbol{u_y} \\ \boldsymbol{u_y} \\ \end{bmatrix} \;\;\;\; \textrm{and} \;\;\;\; \boldsymbol{w} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{e_x} \\ \boldsymbol{e_y} \\ \boldsymbol{e_y} \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

the approximation to the inner product is given by:

\[(\vec{u}, \sigma \vec{w}) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \sigma \vec{w} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\boldsymbol{u} M_{\sigma f}} \, \boldsymbol{w}\]

where the mass matrix has the form:

\[\boldsymbol{M_{\sigma f}} = \frac{1}{4} \boldsymbol{P_f^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \sigma ) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_f}\]

and

  • \(k = 1,2,3\) represent the dimension (1D, 2D or 3D)

  • \(\boldsymbol{e_k}\) is a vector of 1s of length \(k\)

  • \(\odot\) is the Hadamard product

  • \(\otimes\) is the kronecker product

  • \(\boldsymbol{P_f}\) is a projection matrix that maps from faces to cell centers

  • \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is a vector that stores all of the volumes of the cells

  • \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\) is a vector containing the physical property values for the cells

Vectors on Cell Edges¶

We want to approximate the inner product between a vector quantity \(\vec{u}\) and the product of \(\sigma\) and \(\vec{w}\). Here, we discretize such that \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) are defined to live at cell edges. Our goal is to construct the inner product matrix \(\boldsymbol{M}\) in the expression below:

(4)¶\[(\vec{u}, \sigma \vec{w}) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \sigma \vec{w} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M \, w}\]

We must respect the dot product. For vectors defined on cell edges, we discretize such that the x-component of the vectors live on the x-edges, the y-component lives y-edges and the z-component lives on the z-edges. This is illustrated in 2D and 3D below. By decomposing the domain into a set of finite cells, we assume the material properties are spacial invariant within each cell.

As we can see there are 2 edges for each component in 2D and 4 edges for each component in 3D. Therefore, we need to project each component of the vector from its edges to the cell centers and take their averages separately. For a single cell with volume \(v_i\) and material property value \(\sigma_i\), the contribution towards the inner product is:

(5)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \mathbf{In \; 2D:} \; \int_{\Omega_i} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv \approx & \;\; \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_x^{(1)} + u_x^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_x^{(1)} + w_x^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{4} \Big ( u_y^{(1)} + u_y^{(2)} \Big ) \Big ( w_y^{(1)} + w_y^{(2)} \Big ) \\ & \\ \mathbf{In \; 3D:} \; \int_{\Omega_i} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv \approx & \;\; \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{16} \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 u_x^{(n)} \Bigg ) \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 w_x^{(n)} \Bigg ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{16} \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 u_y^{(n)} \Bigg ) \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 w_y^{(n)} \Bigg ) \\ & + \frac{v_i \sigma_i}{16} \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 u_z^{(n)} \Bigg ) \Bigg ( \sum_{n=1}^4 w_z^{(n)} \Bigg ) \end{align}\end{split}\]

where the superscript \((n)\) denotes a particular edge. Using the contribution for each cell described in expression (5), we want to approximate the inner product in the form described by equation (4). To accomlish this, we construct a sparse matrix \(\boldsymbol{P_e}\) which projects quantities on the x, y and z edges separately to the the cell centers.

For discretize vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) whose x, y (and z) components are organized on cell edges as follows:

\[\begin{split}\boldsymbol{u} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{u_x} \\ \boldsymbol{u_y} \\ \boldsymbol{u_y} \\ \end{bmatrix} \;\;\;\; \textrm{and} \;\;\;\; \boldsymbol{w} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{e_x} \\ \boldsymbol{e_y} \\ \boldsymbol{e_y} \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

the approximation to the inner product is given by:

\[(\vec{u}, \vec{w}) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\boldsymbol{u} M_e \, \boldsymbol{w}}\]

where the mass matrix for face quantities has the form:

\[\boldsymbol{M_{\sigma e}} = \frac{1}{4^{k-1}} \boldsymbol{P_e^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \sigma) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_e}\]

and

  • \(k = 1,2,3\) represent the dimension (1D, 2D or 3D)

  • \(\boldsymbol{e_k}\) is a vector of 1s of length \(k\)

  • \(\odot\) is the Hadamard product

  • \(\otimes\) is the kronecker product

  • \(\boldsymbol{P_e}\) is a projection matrix that maps from edges to cell centers

  • \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is a vector that stores all of the volumes of the cells

  • \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\) is a vector containing the physical property values for the cells

Reciprocal Properties¶

Let \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) be two physically related quantities. If their relationship is isotropic and defined by the reciprocal of a physical property (defined by a constant \(\rho\)), then the constitutive relation is given by:

(6)¶\[\vec{v} = \rho^{-1} \vec{w}\]

Because the relationship between \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) is a constant, the derivation of the inner-product matrix at edges and faces is effectively the same. For this case, the corresponding inner product matricies for discrete quantities living on various parts of the mesh are given by:

\[\begin{split}\textrm{Vectors on faces:} \; \boldsymbol{M_{\rho f}} &= \frac{1}{4} \boldsymbol{P_f^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \rho^{-1} ) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_f} \\ \textrm{Vectors on edges:} \; \boldsymbol{M_{\rho e}} &= \frac{1}{4^{k-1}} \boldsymbol{P_e^T } \textrm{diag} \boldsymbol{\big ( e_k \otimes (v \odot \rho^{-1}) \big )} \boldsymbol{P_e}\end{split}\]

where

  • \(k = 1,2,3\) represent the dimension (1D, 2D or 3D)

  • \(\boldsymbol{e_k}\) is a vector of 1s of length \(k\)

  • \(\odot\) is the Hadamard product

  • \(\otimes\) is the kronecker product

  • \(\boldsymbol{P_f}\) and \(\boldsymbol{P_e}\) are projection matricies that map quantities from faces and edges to cell centers, respectively

  • \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is a vector that stores all of the volumes of the cells

  • \(\boldsymbol{\rho^{-1}}\) is a vector containing the reciprocal of \(\rho\) for all cells
