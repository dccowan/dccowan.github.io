Advection and Diffusion with Zero Neumann Boundary Condition¶

Here we provide the derivation for solving the advection-diffusion equation using the finite volume method. We assume the fluid is incompressible. Key lessons include:

  • Implementing boundary conditions

  • Solving time-dependent PDEs

  • Strategies for applying finite volume to 2nd order PDEs

Tutorial: Advection-Diffusion Equation

Setup¶

If we assume the fluid is incompressible (i.e. \(\nabla \cdot \vec{u} = 0\)), the advection-diffusion equation with zero Neumann boundary conditions is given by:

(1)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} & p_t = \nabla \cdot \alpha \nabla p - \vec{u} \cdot \nabla p + s \\ & \textrm{s.t.} \;\;\; \frac{\partial p}{\partial n} \Bigg |_{\partial \Omega} = 0 \\ & \textrm{and} \;\;\; p(t=0) = 0 \end{align}\end{split}\]

where

  • \(p\) is the unknown variable

  • \(p_t\) is its time-derivative

  • \(\alpha\) defines the diffusivity within the domain

  • \(\vec{u}\) is the velocity field

  • \(s\) is the source term

We will consider the case where there is a single point source within our domain. Where \(s_0\) is a constant:

\[s = s_0 \delta ( \vec{r} )\]

Direct implementation of the finite volume method is more challenging on higher order PDEs. Therefore, we redefine the problem as a set of first order PDEs as follows:

(2)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} p_t = \nabla \cdot \vec{j} - \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} + s \;\;\;\; &(1)\\ \vec{w} = \nabla p \;\;\;\; &(2)\\ \alpha^{-1} \vec{j} = \vec{w} \;\;\;\; &(3) \end{align}\end{split}\]

We then take the inner products between the expressions in equation (2) and an appropriate test function.

Discretization in Space¶

Because this is a time-dependent problem, we must consider discretization in both space and time. We generally begin by discretizing in space. Where \(\boldsymbol{p}\) is the discrete representation of \(p\), we will discretize such that \(\boldsymbol{p}\) lives on the cell centers. If \(\boldsymbol{p}\) lives at cell centers, it makes sense for the discrete representations \(\vec{w}\), \(\vec{j}\) and \(\vec{u}\) in expression (2) to live on the faces.

First Equation¶

Let \(\psi\) be a scalar test function whose discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) lives at cell centers. The inner product between \(\psi\) and the first equation in (2) is given by:

(3)¶\[\int_\Omega \psi p_t \, dv = \int_\Omega \psi \nabla \cdot \vec{j} \, dv + \int_\Omega \psi (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv + \int_\Omega \psi s \, dv\]

According to basic inner products, the term to the left of the equals sign is approximated by:

(4)¶\[\int_\Omega \psi p_t \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c \, p_t}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{M_c}\) is the inner product matrix for quantities at cell centers.

Since \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) lives at cell centers, then so must the divergence of \(\vec{j}\). This implies the discrete vector \(\boldsymbol{j}\) must live on the faces. And according to inner products with differential operators:

(5)¶\[\int_\Omega \psi \nabla \cdot \vec{j} \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c D \, j}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is the discrete divergence matrix.

For the next term in equation (3), we must take the dot product of vectors \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{w}\). Here, we let the corresponding discrete representations \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) live on the faces; this is because we need the gradient of \(\boldsymbol{p}\) to live on the faces. In doing so, we need a set of operations which multiplies the components of the dot product and sums them at the cell center. Where \(\boldsymbol{A_{fc}}\) is the scalar averaging matrix from faces to cell centers and \(c\) = 1, 2 or 3 is the dimension of the problem, the inner product is approximated by:

(6)¶\[\int_\Omega \psi (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \approx c \, \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c A_{fc}} diag(\boldsymbol{u}) \, \boldsymbol{w}\]

For the final term in (3), our inner product contains the delta function. As a result:

(7)¶\[\int_\Omega \psi q \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\psi^T q}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{q}\) is a discrete representation for the integrated source term in each cell. In this case, \(\boldsymbol{q_i}=s_0\) at the center of the cell containing the source It is zero for every other cell.

If we substitute the inner product approximations from expressions (4), (5), (6) and (7) into equation (3), we obtain:

(8)¶\[\boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c \, p_t} = \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c D \, j} - c\, \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c A_{fc}} \, diag(\boldsymbol{u}) \, \boldsymbol{w} + \boldsymbol{\psi^T q}\]

Second Equation¶

In the second equation of (2), we use what we learned in inner products with differential operators. Let \(\vec{f}\) be a vector test function whose discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{f}\) lives on the faces. After using the vector identity \(\vec{f} \cdot \nabla p = \nabla \cdot p\vec{f} - p \nabla \cdot \vec{f}\) and applying the divergence theorem:

(9)¶\[\int_\Omega \vec{f} \cdot \vec{w} = - \int_\Omega p \nabla \cdot \vec{f} \, dv + \oint_{\partial \Omega} p \hat{n} \cdot \vec{f} \, da\]

The discrete approximation is given by:

(10)¶\[\boldsymbol{f^T M_f \, w} = - \boldsymbol{f^T D^T M_c \, p + f^T B \, p}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{B}\) is a sparse matrix that imposes boundary conditions correctly on \(p\).

Third Equation¶

In the third equation of (2), we use what we learned in inner products with contitutive relationships; assume the diffusibility \(\alpha\) is linear isotropic. The inner product with a vector test function \(\vec{f}\) whose discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{f}\) lives on the faces is given by:

(11)¶\[\int_\Omega \vec{f} \cdot \alpha^{\! -1} \vec{j} \, dv = \int_\Omega \vec{f} \cdot \vec{w} \, dv\]

Our formulation defines the diffusivity in terms of its inverse. As a result, the approximation of the inner products is given by:

(12)¶\[\boldsymbol{f^T M_\alpha \, j} = \boldsymbol{f^T M_f w}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{M_\alpha}\) is the inner product matrix at faces for the reciprocal of the diffusivity.

Combining the Expressions¶

Here, we substitute the discrete representations of the inner products from expressions (10) and (12) into (8) and factor like-terms.

Let \(\boldsymbol{\tilde{G}}\) represent a modified gradient operator with the boundary conditions implemented:

(13)¶\[\boldsymbol{\tilde{G}} = \boldsymbol{-D^T M_c + B}\]

The system of equations discretized in space is given by:

(14)¶\[\boldsymbol{p_t} = \boldsymbol{\big [ D \, M_\alpha^{-1} \tilde{G}} - c\, \boldsymbol{A_{fc}} diag(\boldsymbol{u}) \, \boldsymbol{M_f^{-1} \tilde{G} \big ] \, p} + \boldsymbol{M_c^{-1} \, q}\]

Discretization in Time¶

To discretize in time, let us re-express equations (14) as:

(15)¶\[\boldsymbol{p_t} = \boldsymbol{- M \, p + s}\]

where

(16)¶\[\boldsymbol{M} = - \boldsymbol{D \, M_\alpha^{-1} \tilde{G}} + c\, \boldsymbol{A_{fc}} diag(\boldsymbol{u}) \, \boldsymbol{M_f^{-1} \tilde{G}}\]

and

(17)¶\[\boldsymbol{s} = \boldsymbol{M_c^{-1} \, q}\]

There are a multitude of ways in which discretization in time can be implemented. A stable and easy method to implement is the backward Euler. By implementing the backward Euler, we must solve the following linear system at each time step \(k\):

(18)¶\[\big [ \boldsymbol{I} + \Delta t \, \boldsymbol{M} \big ] \, \boldsymbol{p}^{k+1} = \boldsymbol{p}^k + \Delta t \, \boldsymbol{s}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{I}\) is the identity matrix and \(\Delta t\) is the step length.
