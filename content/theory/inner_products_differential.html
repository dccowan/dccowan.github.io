Differential Operators¶

Introduction¶

For practical applications of the finite volume method, we may need to take the inner product of expressions containing differential operators. These operators include the:

  • the gradient (\(\nabla \phi\) )

  • the divergence (\(\nabla \cdot \vec{w}\) )

  • the curl (\(\nabla \times \vec{w}\) )

For scalar quantities \(\psi\) and \(\phi\) and for vector quantities \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{w}\) we are interested in approximating the following inner products:

\[\begin{split}\begin{align} (\vec{u}, \nabla \phi ) &= \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv\\ (\psi, \nabla \cdot \vec{w} ) &= \int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \\ (\vec{u}, \nabla \times \vec{w} ) &= \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w} ) \, dv \end{align}\end{split}\]

Tutorial: To construct differential operators and/or approximate inner products of this type, see the tutorial on inner products with differential operators

Gradient¶

For the inner product between a vector \(\vec{u}\) and the gradient of a scalar \(\phi\), there are two options for where the variables should live. For \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) defined on the nodes and \(\boldsymbol{u}\) defined on cell edges:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M_e G \, \phi}\]

And for \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) defined at cell centers and \(\boldsymbol{u}\) defined on cell faces:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv \approx - \boldsymbol{u^T D^T M_c \, \phi} + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{G}\) is a discrete gradient operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is a discrete divergence operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_c}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at cell centers

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_e}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at edges

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\phi\). It is zero when \(\phi = 0\) on the boundary.

Divergence¶

For the inner product between a scalar (\(\psi\)) and the divergence of a vector (\(\vec{w}\)), there are two options for where the variables should live. For \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) defined at cell centers and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) defined on cell faces:

\[\int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c D \, w}\]

And for \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) defined on the nodes and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) defined on cell edges:

\[\int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \approx - \boldsymbol{\psi^T G^T M_e \, w } + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{G}\) is a discrete gradient operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is a discrete divergence operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_c}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at cell centers

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_e}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at edges

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\vec{w}\). It is zero when \(\hat{n} \cdot \vec{w} = 0\) on the boundary.

Curl¶

For the inner product between a vector (\(\vec{u}\)) and the curl of another vector (\(\vec{w}\)), there are two options for where the variables should live. For \(\boldsymbol{u}\) defined on the faces and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) defined on cell edges:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w} ) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M_f C \, w}\]

And for \(\boldsymbol{u}\) defined on the edges and \(\boldsymbol{w}\) defined on cell faces:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w} ) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T C^T \! M_f \, w } + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{C}\) is a discrete curl operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_f}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors on cell faces

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\vec{w}\). It is zero when \(\hat{n} \times \vec{w} = 0\) on the boundary.

Vector and the Gradient of a Scalar¶

Let \(\phi\) be a scalar quantity and let \(\vec{u}\) be a vector quantity. We are interested in approximating the following inner product:

(1)¶\[(\vec{u}, \nabla \phi ) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv\]

Inner Product on Edges¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) lives on the nodes and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{u}\) lives on the edges. Since the discrete gradient operator maps a discrete scalar quantity from nodes to edges, we can approximate the inner product between two discrete quantities living on the edges. Thus:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M_e G \, \phi}\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{G}\) is the discrete gradient operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_e}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at edges

Inner Product on Faces¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) lives at cell centers and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{u}\) lives on the faces. In this case we cannot simply use a discrete gradient operator, as a mapping from cell centers to faces would require knowledge of the scalar at locations outside the mesh.

To evaluate the inner product we use the identity \(\vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi = \nabla \cdot \phi\vec{u} - \phi \nabla \cdot \vec{u}\) and apply the divergence theorem to equation (1):

(2)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv &= - \int_\Omega \phi \nabla \cdot \vec{u} \, dv + \int_\Omega \nabla \cdot \phi\vec{u} \, dv \\ &= - \int_\Omega \phi \nabla \cdot \vec{u} \, dv + \oint_{\partial \Omega} \hat{n} \cdot \phi\vec{u} \, da \\ &= - \int_\Omega \phi \nabla \cdot \vec{u} \, dv + \oint_{\partial \Omega} \phi \hat{n} \cdot \vec{u} \, da \end{align}\end{split}\]

Where boundary conditions are implemented in the surface integral. The approximate to the inner product is given by:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dv \approx - \boldsymbol{u^T D^T M_c \, \phi} + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is the discrete divergence operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_c}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at cell centers

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\phi\)

When formulating the approximation to the inner product in this way, the natural boundary condition is a Dirichlet condition such that \(\phi = 0\) on the boundary. In this case, the added boundary condition term is zero.

Scalar and the Divergence of a Vector¶

Let \(\psi\) be a scalar quantity and let \(\vec{w}\) be a vector quantity. We are interested in approximating the following inner product:

(3)¶\[\begin{split}(\psi, \nabla \cdot \vec{w} ) = \int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \\\end{split}\]

Inner Product on Faces¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) lives at cell centers and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{w}\) lives on the faces. Since the discrete divergence operator maps a discrete vector quantity from faces to cell centers, we can approximate the inner product between two discrete quantities living at the centers. Thus:

\[\int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{\psi^T M_c D \, w}\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is the discrete divergence operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_c}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at cell centers

Inner Product on Edges¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{\psi}\) lives on the nodes and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{w}\) lives on the edges. We cannot simply use a discrete divergence operator, as a mapping from edges to nodes would require knowledge of \(\vec{w}\) at locations outside the mesh.

To evaluate the inner product we use the identity \(\psi \nabla \cdot \vec{w} = \nabla \cdot \psi\vec{w} - \vec{w} \cdot \nabla \psi\) and apply the divergence theorem to equation (1):

(4)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv &= - \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot \nabla \psi \, dv + \int_\Omega \nabla \cdot \psi\vec{w} \, dv \\ &= - \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot \nabla \psi \, dv + \oint_{\partial \Omega} \hat{n} \cdot \psi\vec{w} \, da \\ &= - \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot \nabla \psi \, dv + \oint_{\partial \Omega} \psi (\hat{n} \cdot \vec{w}) \, da \end{align}\end{split}\]

Where boundary conditions are implemented in the surface integral. The approximate to the inner product is given by:

\[\int_\Omega \psi \; (\nabla \cdot \vec{w}) \, dv \approx - \boldsymbol{\psi^T G^T M_e \, w} + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{G}\) is the discrete gradient operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_e}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors at the edges

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\vec{w}\)

When formulating the approximation to the inner product in this way, the natural boundary condition is for \(\hat{n} \cdot \vec{w} = 0\) on the boundary. In this case, the added boundary condition term is zero.

Vector and the Curl of a Vector¶

Let \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{w}\) be vector quantities. We are interested in approximating the following inner product:

(5)¶\[(\vec{u}, \nabla \times \vec{w} ) = \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w} ) \, dv\]

Inner Product at Faces¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{u}\) lives on the faces and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{w}\) lives on the edges. Since the discrete curl operator maps a discrete vector quantity from edges to faces, we can approximate the inner product between two discrete quantities living on the faces. Thus:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w}) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T M_f C w}\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{C}\) is the discrete curl operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_f}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors on faces

Inner Product at Edges¶

Here, the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{u}\) lives on the edges and the discrete representation \(\boldsymbol{w}\) lives on the faces. We cannot simply use the discrete curl operator, as a mapping from faces to edges would require knowledge of \(\boldsymbol{w}\) at locations outside the mesh.

To evaluate the inner product we use the identity \(\vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w}) = \vec{w} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{u}) - \nabla \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{w})\) and apply the divergence theorem to equation (5):

(6)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w}) \, dv &= \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{u}) \, dv - \int_\Omega \nabla \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{w}) \, dv \\ &= \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{u}) \, dv - \oint_{\partial \Omega} \hat{n} \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{w}) \, da \\ &= \int_\Omega \vec{w} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{u}) \, dv - \oint_{\partial \Omega} (\vec{u} \times \vec{w}) \cdot d\vec{a} \end{align}\end{split}\]

Where boundary conditions are implemented in the surface integral. The approximate to the inner product is given by:

\[\int_\Omega \vec{u} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{w}) \, dv \approx \boldsymbol{u^T C^T M_f \, w} + B.\! C.\]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{C}\) is the discrete curl operator

  • \(\boldsymbol{M_f}\) is the basic inner product matrix for vectors on faces

  • \(B.\! C.\) represents an additional term that must be constructed to impose boundary conditions correctly on \(\vec{w}\)

When formulating the approximation to the inner product in this way, the natural boundary condition is for \(\hat{n} \times \vec{w} = 0\) on the boundary. In this case, the added boundary condition term is zero.
